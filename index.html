Saturn

Saturn is the 6th planet from the sun and the second-largest in our solar system after Jupiter. It is a gas giant whose average radius is about nine times that of Earth. It is named for the ancient Roman god of agriculture, father to Jupiter and consort to Lua, and Saturn’s astronomical symbol is derived from his sickle.

Saturn’s core is surrounded by a deep layer of metallic hydrogen, an intermediate layer of liquid hydrogen and liquid helium, and finally a gaseous outer layer.

Saturn has a prominent ring system that consists of nine continuous main rings that is composed mostly of ice particles, with a smaller amount of rocky debris and dust. Sixty-two moons are known to orbit Saturn, of which fifty-three are officially named. Titan, Saturn’s largest moon, is the only moon in the Solar System to have a substantial atmosphere.

Its Roman namesake is the god of agriculture who oversaw the Golden Age, where Hesiod and Ovid described humans enjoying the bounty of the earth without farming or labouring.

He arrived in Italy “de-throned and fugitive”, but brought agriculture and prosperity. His consort Lua, “Mother Destruction”, was a foil for his benevolence and the weapons of enemies were burned in her name.

Tartarus / TITANOMACHY

Tartarus is the place that deities, demi-gods, and monsters are sent to be punished or imprisoned. Like many concepts in Greek mythology, it is understood as both a physical thing or place, and a godly force. It is even deeper inside the Earth than the Underworld governed by Hades, and in some tellings of myth it is Gaia’s womb, wherein her undesirable children are put back inside of her. Along that vein, ancient Orphic texts understood Tartarus as the “unbounded first-existing entity from which the Light and the Cosmos [were] born”.

The most well-known inhabitants of Tartarus are the giants and titans. Ouranos (Uranus, god of The Sky) and Gaia (goddess of The Earth, The Deep) had titan and giant children. Shocked and disgusted by the giants’ ugliness, Ouranos put them back inside Gaia and imprisoned them in Tartarus. The giants themselves were the one-eyed Cyclopes and the hundred-armed Hekatonkheires. Injured and scorned, Gaia incensed her titan children to usurp Ouranos and free their siblings. The titan Cronus took charge and laid an ambush, capturing Ouranos on his nightly descent to embrace Gaia. The giants were freed while he was pinned, and Ouranos was castrated before being overthrown. Curiously, after taking power, Cronus re-imprisoned the giants in Tartarus.

Ouranos prophesized that Cronus would be overthrown in turn by one of his own children. Cronus consumed every single one of his offspring as a preventative measure but the titan Rhea, his consort and mother to most of his children, managed to save one child. She fed Cronus a blanket-wrapped stone in place of the baby she had smuggled away to a foster mother.

This child grew up to be Zeus, and Gaia saw another chance to free her imprisoned children. She encouraged Zeus to break them out of Tartarus and recruit them as powerful allies with which to defeat Cronus, fulfilling Ouranos’s prophecy. Zeus had to defeat the she-dragon stationed as Tartarus’s guard, Kampe, and together he and the giants waged war on the titans, an event known as the Titanomachy. They were successful; Zeus’s siblings were freed from Cronus’s belly and the titans were imprisoned in Tartarus.

In common Buddhist belief, the malevolent god Mara has three daughters, whom he either sent to tempt Gautama Buddha or went of their own accord, depending on the telling. They were Tanhā (Craving), Raga (Attachment), and Arati (Aversion). They represent both physical and metaphorical entities, being beautiful women as well as the Three Poisons that frustrate enlightenment.

Tanhā represents the black hole of need that an unhappy individual will always being trying to fill. Mara

In Buddhism, Mara is acknowledged as both a physical and metaphorical entity just as his daughters are. He has domain over the inferior realms of Kāmadhātu, where he can influence the beings born into an ‘unhappy’ rebirth such as Asura, Preta, and animals. Mara is infamous for tempting Gautama Buddha in an effort to prevent his englightenment and keep him trapped in the endless cycle of death and rebirth.

Mara’s name comes from the Proto-Indo-European root mer, meaning “to die”. As a verbal noun his name means “causing death” or “killing”. While there are some parallels between Mara’s role and the roles of death gods such as Thanatos or the Grim Reaper, Mara is better known for perpetuating death by distracting souls from enlightenment with lust, desire, pride, and other passions.

Asura

In Buddhism, Asura are the lowest-ranking of the demigods residing in Kāmadhātu. The word is sometimes translated to “titan” or “anti-god” and has links to the name of the hundred-armed giants in Greek myth, Hekatonkheires. Asura have three faces, four to six arms, and are characterized by their addiction to passions such as lust, pride, falseness, hubris, and envy — particularly envy for the devas, beings slightly above Asura in status.

Asura are mostly harmless besides those residing in the realms influenced by Mara, whose corruption makes Asura combative and malevolent.

Asura are tribal deities and their faction leaders are called Asurendes (“Asura-lord”).

One of the most well-known Asurendes was Vemacitrin, who lead the charge up the mountain Sumeru. The Asura used to live at Sumeru’s peak, and when Sakra became its new ruler, the Asura celebrated heartily with forbidden wine. Eris / Enyo / Discordia / Strife

The many-named Greek goddess Eris, Enyo, Discordia, Strife, whose wrath is relentless, she is only a little thing at the first, but thereafter grows until she strides on the earth with her head striking heaven. She hurls down bitterness equally between both sides as she walks through the onslaught, making men’s pain heavier.

At the wedding of of Peleus and Thetis, to which she was not invited, Eris tossed into the party the Apple of Discord, a golden apple inscribed “For the fairest”, provoking the goddesses to begin quarreling about the appropriate recipient. The hapless Paris, Prince of Troy, was appointed to select the fairest by Zeus. The goddesses stripped naked to try to win Paris’ decision, and also attempted to bribe him.

Hera offered political power.

Athena promised infinite wisdom.

Aphrodite tempted him with the most beautiful woman in the world: Helen, wife of Menelaus of Sparta.

Paris chose to award the apple to Aphrodite, forsaking the virtues of might and knowledge, thereby dooming Troy in the war that ensued.
